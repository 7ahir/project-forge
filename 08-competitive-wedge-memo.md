# Competitive Wedge Memo

## Question This File Answers

Where would I choose to win in the AI coding assistant category, and what would I refuse to chase too early?

## PM Skills Demonstrated

- `positioning-statement`: sharpens where the product should compete and why it should be chosen
- `company-research`: turns category patterns into strategic choices without relying on private detail
- `product-strategy-session`: connects wedge choice to roadmap, GTM, and adoption consequences

## Thesis

The strongest wedge in this category is not "AI that can write code."

That is table stakes.

The stronger wedge is:

- trusted repo-aware edits
- useful debug and test repair support
- review-ready change explanation
- enough team visibility to expand adoption without killing speed

That wedge is narrower than "AI for all software engineering," but it is more defensible because it sits inside repeated, trust-sensitive workflows.

## The Competitive Landscape I Would Use

I would not reason about competitors only as named companies. I would reason about them as category archetypes.

| Archetype | Typical strength | Typical weakness |
|---|---|---|
| Benchmark-first assistant | Strong launch narrative and raw generation demos | Weak workflow truth in live repos |
| IDE convenience assistant | Fast, easy, broadly accessible | Hard to differentiate if depth and trust are weak |
| Enterprise governance-first assistant | Good buying story for leadership | Risk of weak developer love or heavy product feel |
| Agentic coding assistant | Strong autonomy narrative | Trust and control often lag behind ambition |

## Where I Would Choose To Win

### Wedge 1: Edit-to-PR reliability

I would aim to become the assistant developers trust to move from task to review-ready change.

That means:

- better repo context
- better diffs
- better test awareness
- better review and PR support

This is more defensible than generic drafting because it compounds into habit and team trust.

### Wedge 2: Trust visible in product behavior

I would compete on inspectability, reversibility, context transparency, and workflow-level proof.

That is harder to copy than marketing language about "quality."

### Wedge 3: Team expansion without enterprise drag

I would make the product easy for a strong individual developer to love, but easier for a tech lead to standardize once value is proven.

That creates a better bridge from pull to revenue.

## What I Would Not Chase First

- broad "AI for the whole SDLC" storytelling
- many thin assistant surfaces across every developer tool
- highly autonomous agent behavior before the edit-to-PR loop is trusted
- governance-heavy enterprise packaging before workflow value is clear

These can all become roadmap debt if they arrive before the wedge is earned.

## Why This Wedge Is Defensible

### 1. It sits in repeated workflows

Developers do not only need code generation. They need help getting work finished, checked, and reviewed.

### 2. It compounds into team trust

If the assistant improves the review-ready quality of changes, the value is visible to more than one user.

### 3. It gives GTM a better proof story

"This helps your team ship review-ready changes faster" is stronger than "our model is excellent."

### 4. It creates a harder roadmap for competitors to copy superficially

Competitors can copy a feature label faster than they can copy reliable repo-context handling, useful diffs, and review trust.

## Strategy Consequences

| Choice | Consequence |
|---|---|
| Choose edit-to-PR wedge | Protect repo, test, and review quality ahead of broad feature expansion |
| Choose trust-visible product behavior | Design for inspectability and reversibility, not just speed |
| Choose team expansion bridge | Build team visibility only after individual workflow value is real |

## Anti-Patterns

### Feature-parity chasing

Trying to match every competitor surface weakens the product thesis.

### Marketing wedge without product wedge

If the product cannot prove its differentiator inside real workflows, the wedge is just copywriting.

### Enterprise-first abstraction

If the product gets heavy before developers trust it, expansion becomes harder, not easier.

## What This Proves In A Hiring Process

- I know how to choose where to compete instead of describing the whole category.
- I understand that wedge choice must shape roadmap and GTM, not just positioning copy.
- I can say no to obvious but lower-quality strategy paths.
